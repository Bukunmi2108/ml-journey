## Overview

In the first part of the project, we priced options under models that relax the constant-volatility assumption. We used the Heston model for the stochastic volatility part and the Merton jump-diffusion model for the jump-risk part.

The shared inputs were an initial stock price of 80, a risk-free rate of 5.5%, time to maturity of 0.25 years, and an ATM strike of 80. For Heston, the variance process used v0 = 0.032, kappa = 1.85, theta = 0.045, and xi = 0.35. For Merton, the diffusion volatility was 35%, with jump mean -0.5 and jump volatility 0.22.

All prices were estimated by Monte Carlo simulation. We used 250,000 paths, and for the path-dependent questions we used 126 time steps. We also kept the Monte Carlo standard errors in the tables. They are not part of the final option price, but they help explain why the parity checks are close rather than exact.

## Question 5

Using the Heston model with correlation -0.30, the ATM European option prices were:

| Option | Strike | Price | Monte Carlo SE |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Call | 80.00 | 3.47 | 0.0093 |
| Put | 80.00 | 2.37 | 0.0080 |

For this run, the correlation between the stock return shock and the variance shock was moderately negative. We included this point because the negative correlation is what produces the leverage effect in the Heston simulation.

The call price came out higher than the put price. That is not surprising here because the risk-free rate is positive, and both options have the same strike and maturity. The put still has a meaningful value because the stock can still finish below the strike, especially when volatility is allowed to move during the life of the option.

Changing the correlation from -0.30 to -0.70 makes the leverage effect stronger. In other words, the simulation links falling stock prices more strongly with rising variance. The put price increased slightly, which fits the intuition that stronger downside-volatility behavior should help the put.

The price change was not large. Since the maturity is only three months and the option is at the money, most of the effect shows up in the risk sensitivities rather than in a large change in the option price.

## Question 7

The finite-difference Delta and Gamma estimates for the Heston options were:

| Correlation | Option | Price | Delta | Gamma |
| ---: | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| -0.30 | Call | 3.47 | 0.6037 | 0.0545 |
| -0.30 | Put | 2.37 | -0.3964 | 0.0545 |
| -0.70 | Call | 3.50 | 0.6322 | 0.0498 |
| -0.70 | Put | 2.41 | -0.3678 | 0.0498 |

The Delta estimates also worked as a check on the simulation. The call Deltas are positive because calls gain when the stock rises. The put Deltas are negative because puts lose value when the stock rises.

One useful check is that the call and put Deltas are linked in a sensible way. For each correlation case, the call Delta is about one more than the put Delta. This is what we expect for European options with the same strike and maturity.

Gamma was positive in each case. This tells us that the option value is curved with respect to the stock price, so a hedge based on Delta would need to be updated as the stock moves. In our results, the stronger negative correlation increased the call Delta but slightly reduced Gamma.

## Question 8

Using the Merton model with jump intensity 0.75, the ATM European option prices were:

| Option | Strike | Price | Monte Carlo SE |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Call | 80.00 | 8.34 | 0.0231 |
| Put | 80.00 | 7.21 | 0.0246 |

With jump intensity 0.75, jumps are relatively more likely during the three-month period. This made the Merton prices much higher than the Heston ATM prices. The reason is that jumps widen the range of possible terminal stock prices.

This wider range helps both option types. The call benefits from large upward outcomes, and the put benefits from large downward outcomes. The model also includes the drift adjustment for jumps, so the higher prices are not just caused by pushing the average stock path up. They come from the extra spread in the payoff distribution.

## Question 9

Using the Merton model with jump intensity 0.25, the ATM European option prices were:

| Option | Strike | Price | Monte Carlo SE |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Call | 80.00 | 6.81 | 0.0202 |
| Put | 80.00 | 5.73 | 0.0185 |

When the jump intensity was reduced to 0.25, both option prices fell. This was the expected result because fewer jumps mean less chance of extreme terminal stock values.

Even with the lower jump intensity, the Merton prices were still higher than the Heston ATM prices from Questions 5 and 6. That is an important difference between the two models in this project. Heston changes the volatility path, but Merton adds discontinuous moves, and those jumps add value to options because the payoff is asymmetric.

## Question 10

The finite-difference Delta and Gamma estimates for the Merton options were:

| Jump Intensity | Option | Price | Delta | Gamma |
| ---: | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 0.75 | Call | 8.34 | 0.6495 | 0.0226 |

| 0.25 | Call | 6.81 | 0.5962 | 0.0269 |
| 0.25 | Put | 5.73 | -0.4036 | 0.0269 |

The Delta signs again match the option payoff logic. Calls have positive Delta and puts have negative Delta. With jump intensity 0.75, the call Delta is higher and the put Delta is less negative than in the 0.25 case.

The Gamma values are positive, but they are lower than the Heston Gamma values in our tables. This is a useful reminder that a higher option price does not automatically mean a higher Gamma. The Merton model gives higher prices because of jump tails, while Gamma is a local measure around the current stock price.

## Question 11

Put-call parity was checked using:

Call minus put equals S0 minus K times exp(-rT).

The right-hand side is 1.0925.

| Model | Parameter | Call - Put | Parity RHS | Absolute Difference | Conclusion |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Heston | rho = -0.30 | 1.1015 | 1.0925 | 0.0090 | Holds within Monte Carlo error |
| Heston | rho = -0.70 | 1.0932 | 1.0925 | 0.0007 | Holds within Monte Carlo error |
| Merton | lambda = 0.75 | 1.1278 | 1.0925 | 0.0353 | Holds within Monte Carlo error |
| Merton | lambda = 0.25 | 1.0801 | 1.0925 | 0.0124 | Holds within Monte Carlo error |

This was one of the main checks on the calculations. If call minus put were far away from the parity value, then something would probably be wrong with the pricing setup, the discounting, or the payoff calculations.

The results are close to the theoretical value. The largest gap is in the Merton lambda = 0.75 case. That also makes sense because this case had the widest payoff distribution and higher Monte Carlo standard errors. Overall, the parity check supports the European option prices used in the report.

The first thing to notice is that the call prices fall as the strike increases. This matched the usual call option relationship, since a higher strike makes exercise less likely. The Merton call prices are higher than the Heston call prices at every strike in this sweep. The difference is especially clear for the out-of-the-money calls. This is where jump risk matters a lot, because a jump can move the stock into the payoff region more easily than a smooth diffusion path.

## Question 14

The Heston up-and-in call uses correlation -0.70, strike 95, and barrier 95.

| Option | Parameter | Strike | Barrier | Barrier Option Price | Comparable Vanilla Price | Barrier Hit Rate | Comment |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Up-and-in call | rho = -0.70 | 95.00 | 95.00 | 0.03 | 0.03 | 0.0224 | Same as vanilla because barrier equals strike and final monitoring is included |

The up-and-in call only pays if the barrier is reached before maturity and the option finishes in the money. Here the strike and barrier are both 95. If the option finishes in the money, the stock must be above 95 at maturity, so the path has necessarily reached the 95 barrier.

This is why the up-and-in call and the comparable vanilla call have the same price in our result. The hit rate is only 2.24%, which explains why the value is very small, but the paths that create a positive call payoff are already knocked in.

## Question 15

The Merton down-and-in put uses jump intensity 0.75, strike 65, and barrier 65.

| Option | Parameter | Strike | Barrier | Barrier Option Price | Comparable Vanilla Price | Barrier Hit Rate | Comment |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Down-and-in put | lambda = 0.75 | 65.00 | 65.00 | 2.78 | 2.78 | 0.2579 | Same as vanilla because barrier equals strike and final monitoring is included |

The same logic applies to the down-and-in put. The strike and barrier are both 65. If the put ends in the money, then the stock must be below 65 at maturity. That means the lower barrier has been reached.

The down-and-in put therefore has the same value as the comparable vanilla put. The hit rate is 25.79%, which is much higher than the up-and-in call hit rate from Question 14. This fits the Merton setup because downward jumps make it easier for the stock path to touch the lower barrier.

## Conclusion

In these simulations, the Heston and Merton models changed the option prices in different ways. In the Heston section, the stronger negative correlation did not create a large price change, but it did change the Greeks. That matters for hedging because Greeks are what drive the hedge adjustments.

In the Merton section, jump intensity had a clearer effect on the prices. Increasing lambda from 0.25 to 0.75 increased both call and put values. The strike sweep also showed that the jump model gave higher call prices across all strikes, especially for out-of-the-money calls.

The checks support the final results. Put-call parity was close in all four European pricing cases, the call prices declined as strikes increased, and the barrier results followed directly from the fact that the barrier and strike were equal. From our results, Heston mainly changed the volatility and Greek behavior, while Merton's jump intensity had the stronger effect on tail-sensitive prices.
